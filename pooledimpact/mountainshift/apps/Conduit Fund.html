Company Form — How to Use

The Companies screen tracks Sleeve B portfolio companies and their investment structures. Fields show and hide based on the Investment Type you select.

Investment Types
Royalty A % of revenue paid to the fund. Requires Annual Revenue + Royalty Rate.
Revenue-Based A lump-sum advance repaid as a multiple of revenue. Requires Annual Revenue + Revenue Multiple.
Debt A loan with defined principal, return rate, and term.
Equity A perpetual ownership stake. Requires Equity Stake % + Company Valuation.
Hybrid (Debt + Equity) Loan that converts or co-exists with an equity stake.
Hybrid (Royalty + Equity) Royalty stream plus a perpetual equity stake.
Hybrid (Revenue-Based + Equity) Revenue advance plus equity ownership.
Key Fields
Annual Revenue — The company's top-line revenue; used for Royalty and Revenue-Based value models.
Royalty Rate (%) — The % of revenue the fund receives (e.g. 4.5%).
Revenue Multiple (×) — The return cap expressed as a multiple of the advance (e.g. 3.5×).
Loan Principal — Outstanding debt face value for Debt and Hybrid types.
Equity Stake (%) — The fund's ownership percentage in the company.
Company Valuation — Current pre-money or post-money valuation for equity pricing.
EBITDA Margin (%) — Informational; helps contextualize operational health.

LTV — How It's Used

Loan-to-Value (LTV) is the ratio of the outstanding loan balance to the appraised value of the underlying asset. It is expressed as a percentage.

LTV = Loan Balance ÷ Asset Value × 100
LTV by Investment Type
RoyaltyLTV is secondary — yield coverage and royalty rate matter more than collateral ratio. Still captured for the Senior Lender's diligence package.
Revenue-BasedLTV is less relevant; revenue multiple and coverage are primary drivers of recovery analysis.
DebtPrimary credit metric. Determines recovery in a default scenario. Target ≤ 80% for Senior Facility eligibility under the 95% LTV Senior Lender structure.
EquityEquity sits below all debt. A high LTV on the project means equity is deeply subordinated and has lower expected recovery.
Fund Context
The Conduit Fund I Senior Facility supports a 95% LTV against Sleeve A assets, backed by the $9.75M HELOC on Sleeve C real estate as a break-glass liquidity bridge. SPV-level LTV data feeds the quarterly Senior Lender compliance package.

Understanding the Hybrid Investment Report

? How to read this report

This report models the total estimated value of a hybrid investment — one that combines a contractual debt obligation with a perpetual equity stake. The two components are economically distinct and are modeled separately. The figures shown are not a guarantee of return. The debt component reflects a contractual repayment schedule. The equity component reflects a point-in-time valuation mark that will change as the company grows or declines.

Debt Component — Contractual

The debt component represents the total cash the fund expects to receive back from the loan — principal plus interest. It is the most certain element of the modeled value. It does not change based on company performance and it does not compound.

Total Repayment = Principal × (1 + Return Rate × Term)
Example: $5M × (1 + 0.30 × 2) = $8,000,000

Note — Simple vs. Compound Interest: This model uses simple interest, not compound. A $5M loan at 30% over 2 years returns $8M under simple interest. Under annual compounding it would return $8.45M. For terms of 2 years or less the difference is modest but material on large principals. This is disclosed here so you can apply a manual adjustment if your deal terms specify compounding.

Equity Component — Probabilistic

The equity component represents the fund's ownership stake marked at the current company valuation. It is the least certain element of the modeled value. It will increase if the company grows and decrease if it does not.

Equity Value = Stake (%) × Company Valuation
Example: 20% × $20M = $4,000,000

Important: The debt component models a future cash flow (money to be received over the loan term). The equity component models a present-day mark (what the stake is worth today). Strictly speaking, a full analysis would discount both to a common time horizon. This report does not do that — see the Limitations tab for details.

Return Decomposition — Donut Chart

Shows what percentage of the total modeled value comes from the debt component versus the equity component. This immediately communicates the character of the deal: is this primarily a lending position with an equity kicker, or a genuine equity play with debt protection?

A deal that is 80% debt / 20% equity carries very different risk and return characteristics than one that is 40% / 60%. The donut makes that structural split visible at a glance.

Cash Flow Waterfall

Shows the timeline of the investment: capital deployed, debt repayments received by year, and the equity mark at the end of the debt term. This answers the most important question for a hybrid deal — when does the fund get whole on the debt, and what does it still own after that?

The waterfall reveals whether the equity stake is an upside option after a nearly full debt recovery, or a meaningful component of the return from the beginning. These are fundamentally different investment structures even if the total modeled value is similar.

Equity Projection — Scenario Chart

Shows how the equity component could grow over time under Bear, Base, and Bull scenarios. This is powered by the growth rate assumptions you enter — it is explicitly forward-looking and based entirely on your inputs, not verified company data.

The debt component does not appear in this chart because it does not grow — it follows a fixed repayment schedule regardless of company performance. See the Growth Rate Assumptions tab for guidance on setting scenario inputs.

Sensitivity Table

Shows total modeled value across a range of exit valuations, holding the debt component constant. Answers the question: how sensitive is the overall return to the equity outcome?

If the table shows nearly identical totals across a wide valuation range, this is primarily a debt deal and the equity is incidental. If the numbers diverge significantly, the equity component is doing real work in the return profile and deserves more diligence attention.

Growth rate assumptions power the equity projection chart and scenario analysis. They are your inputs, not verified data. The quality of the projection output is entirely dependent on the quality of the assumptions you provide.

Growth Rate by Investment Type
Debt + Equity Growth rate applies only to the equity component. The debt repayment schedule is contractually fixed and is not affected by the growth rate input under any scenario.
Royalty + Equity Growth rate applies to revenue (driving the royalty income stream) and separately to company valuation (driving the equity mark). These are economically distinct drivers and may warrant separate inputs in a full model.
Revenue-Based + Equity Growth rate affects repayment velocity — faster growth means faster repayment, improving IRR even though the total nominal return is capped at the revenue multiple. It also compounds the equity mark. Both effects work in the same direction.
Scenario Calibration Guide
Bear Case
Use a growth rate at or below the industry average for this sector. Ask: what if this company underperforms our thesis? This is the scenario you stress-test loan covenants and payment schedules against.
Base Case
Use the growth rate that reflects your actual investment thesis — the rate you underwrote when you made the deal. This is the number you are accountable to and should be defensible to the investment committee.
Bull Case
Use a growth rate that reflects strong but plausible outperformance. It should be achievable, not aspirational. Avoid rates that require exceptional circumstances — the Bull case is not a best-case fantasy, it is the upper bound of a realistic range.
Saved Scenarios. All assumption inputs are session-only by default. When saved as a named scenario, they are stored in the Company Reports table and can be retrieved, compared, and updated over time. Saved scenarios form the basis for portfolio-level NAV projections across Sleeve B — and eventually across all three sleeves as the platform evolves.

Understanding what this report does not do is as important as understanding what it does. These are not disclaimers for legal protection — they are analytical boundaries you should keep in mind when presenting or acting on these numbers.

Not a DCF Analysis

This report does not discount cash flows to present value. The debt component is shown as a total nominal repayment obligation. The equity component is shown as a present-day mark. Both are modeled at face value — debt at total repayment, equity at current valuation. This is appropriate for a portfolio tracking tool but should not substitute for a full discounted cash flow model when making investment decisions or presenting to institutional counterparties.

No Default Risk Modeling

This report assumes the borrower makes all debt payments as scheduled. It does not stress-test the debt component against revenue shortfalls, covenant breaches, liquidity events, or insolvency scenarios. The Bear Case scenario models slow growth — it does not model non-payment. Default risk must be assessed separately through credit analysis.

Simple Interest on Debt

The debt component uses simple interest: Principal × (1 + Rate × Term). If your deal terms specify annual or monthly compounding, the actual repayment obligation will be higher. For a $5M loan at 30% over 2 years: simple interest returns $8.00M, annual compounding returns $8.45M — a $450K difference on this principal. Verify your deal terms and adjust accordingly.

Mixed Time Horizons

The debt component models future cash flows to be received over the loan term. The equity component models the present-day mark. These two numbers exist at different points in time and are added together without time-value adjustment. A rigorous analysis would bring both to a common date — either discounting the debt repayments to today, or projecting the equity mark forward to the loan maturity date. This report does neither.

Growth Assumptions Are Yours

The model has no ability to validate whether your growth rate assumptions are realistic. It will compute a projection from any number you enter, including implausible ones. The Base Case growth rate you store in the Company Reports table should be treated as a recorded investment thesis commitment — something you revisit and update as actual company performance data becomes available.
